French | Transliteration: Quand on parle du loup, on en voit la queue.
Quand on parle du loup, on en voit la queue.
When you speak of the wolf, you see its tail.
Said when the person or thing being discussed appears or occurs shortly after being mentioned. (Similar to "speak of the devil").
Arabic | Transliteration: al-Kalb al-hayy khayr min al-asad al-mayyit.
الكلب الحي خير من الأسد الميت.
A living dog is better than a dead lion.
Even a humble or weak person who is alive is better off than a strong and powerful person who is dead. Emphasizes that being alive, regardless of status, is paramount.
Chinese | Transliteration: Gǒu zuǐ lǐ tǔ bù chū xiàngyá.
狗嘴里吐不出象牙.
You can't spit ivory out of a dog's mouth.
Someone with a coarse or vulgar nature cannot say refined or elegant things. Reflects on the inherent character of a person.
Italian | Transliteration: Can che abbaia non morde.
Can che abbaia non morde.
Dog that barks does not bite.
Those who are loudest in their threats are often harmless.
Hindi/Urdu | Transliteration: Kutte ki dum thedi ki thedi.
कुत्ते की दुम टेढ़ी की टेढ़ी
A dog's tail remains crooked as it is.
It is impossible to change a person's basic nature or ingrained habits, especially bad ones, no matter how much you try.
Italian | Transliteration: Il lupo perde il pelo ma non il vizio.
Il lupo perde il pelo ma non il vizio.
The wolf loses its fur but not its vice.
People may change their appearance or habits to some extent, but their fundamental negative traits or bad habits remain.
