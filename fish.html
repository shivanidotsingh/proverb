Arabic (Egyptian) | Transliteration: Man yulqá fī an-Nīl maḥẓūẓan wa yakhruju bi samakah fī famih.
من يلقى في النيل محظوظا ويخرج بسمكة في فمه
Throw a fortunate man in the Nile and he will come up with fish in his mouth.
Describes someone with extreme good luck or fortune, suggesting they will find success or benefit even in the most unlikely or difficult circumstances.
Swedish
Man måste kasta ut masken för att fånga fisken.
One must cast out the worm to catch the fish.
You must make an effort or invest something (time, resources, risk) to achieve a desired result or gain something valuable.
Swahili | Transliteration: Samaki huliwa na mwenzake.
Samaki huliwa na mwenzake.
A fish is eaten by its fellow.
Suggests that people are often harmed, exploited, or brought down by those within their own group or community.
Yoruba | Transliteration: Ẹja tí yóò tóbi a láti inú omi ni ó ti ń bẹ̀rẹ̀.
Ẹja tí yóò tóbi a láti inú omi ni ó ti ń bẹ̀rẹ̀.
The fish that will be great starts from inside the water.
Greatness, potential, or significant development begins in one's natural environment or from humble beginnings.
Bengali | Transliteration: Jôler mach dangay uthle bãche na.
জলের মাছ ডাঙায় উঠলে বাঁচে না.
Fish of the water does not survive if it comes ashore.
One cannot survive or thrive outside of their natural or accustomed environment, social setting, or area of expertise.
Italian

The big fish eats the small one.
The powerful, wealthy, or influential tend to dominate, exploit, or consume the weak, poor, or less powerful.
Greek | Transliteration: Apó tin kefalí vromáei to psári.

Problems, corruption, or decay in an organization, system, or group originate from the leadership.
